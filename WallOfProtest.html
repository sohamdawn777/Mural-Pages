The Wall of Protest

Rough, dripping lines, clenched fists, faceless figures—this wall screams rather than speaks. Painted on College Street, this graffiti recalls Kolkata’s long history of student protests, worker uprisings, and Naxalite movements.

The raised fists, marked with “32,” could reference Ward No. 32 of Kolkata Municipal Corporation, a hub of leftist activity. In the late 1960s and ’70s, this very area saw Naxalite youths clash with the police, demanding justice for peasants and workers. Even earlier, in the 1940s, these streets echoed with cries against British rule.

The faceless woman with chains may represent the eternal struggle of Bengal’s women—at once revered as Durga, yet bound in chains of patriarchy. The skeletal forms recall famine and poverty, horrors Bengal knows too well.
