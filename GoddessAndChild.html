The Goddess and the Child

A child in tattered shorts stands with a hoop, framed against a bright mural of Durga atop her lion, flanked by golden wings.

This artwork captures Kolkata’s starkest contrast. The goddess—venerated every autumn with grand pandals and lights—watches over children who often play barefoot in gutters. Historically, Durga in Bengal has been more than a goddess: she was invoked in the nationalist movement as the “Mother of Bengal”, inspiring the likes of Bankim Chandra’s Vande Mataram. Yet here, she is just paint on a wall, while real children grapple with hunger and poverty.

This juxtaposition is the eternal paradox of Kolkata: a city where gods are worshipped with gold, but children on the streets grow up with rusted hoops. And yet, the child smiles faintly—as if knowing the goddess is his guardian too, painted or not.
